# Brief

> [!example] #### Goal 1 - 💳 Face the (Financial) Music
> - By the end of Month 2, I will have a complete, written record of every debt I owe — who it's owed to, how much, the interest rate, and the minimum payment — so that I am making decisions based on reality, not anxiety-driven avoidance.

- **ADHD adjustment:** Financial avoidance is extremely common with ADHD — not opening letters, not checking bank accounts, not adding up the total because the number feels too frightening. But avoidance makes debt worse, not better. This goal is purely about _information gathering_, not fixing anything yet. Just looking. That's it.
- **Visualise it:** Picture sitting with a complete list in front of you — and instead of dread, feeling a strange sense of relief. Because now you know exactly what you're dealing with. The unknown is always scarier than the known. Numbers can be worked with. Fog cannot.
- **Manageable steps:**
    - Week 1: Write down every debt you can recall from memory — credit cards, loans, money owed to people, buy now pay later, overdrafts, everything
    - Week 2: Gather any letters, emails, or statements you've been avoiding — open every single one and add to the list
    - Week 3: Check your credit report for free (Experian, ClearScore, or Credit Karma in the UK) — this will show debts you may have forgotten or lost track of
    - Week 4: Organise the list into a simple table — creditor, total owed, interest rate, minimum payment, status (active, defaulted, in collections)
- **Potential problem:** Emotional overwhelm causing shutdown mid-process — do this in short bursts, 20 minutes at a time. After each session, do something grounding. You don't have to do it all in one sitting.
- **Important:** If any debts have escalated to bailiffs or court orders, contact StepChange (free UK debt charity, 0800 138 1111) or National Debtline immediately — these need specialist guidance, not just a goal-setting framework.
- **Reward:** Once the full list is complete — genuinely celebrate it. You did one of the hardest things a person in debt can do. You looked. That deserves acknowledgement.
