# Brief 

> [!example] #### Goal 2 - 🚭 Quit Smoking & Vaping
> - By December 2026, I will fully quit both smoking and vaping — using a structured reduction plan in the first 3 months, transitioning to a quit date by Month 4, and remaining smoke and vape free for the remainder of the year — tracking usage honestly from day one.

- **ADHD adjustment:** Cold turkey is statistically harder for ADHD brains because nicotine has genuinely been used as self-medication — it temporarily boosts dopamine and focus, which is why the pull is stronger than average. A gradual reduction plan respects that reality without using it as an excuse to never quit. The goal is a clean quit date, reached intelligently.
- **Suggested approach — 3 phase plan:**
    - **Phase 1 — Reduce (Month 1–3):** Track every cigarette and vaping session honestly. Set a weekly reduction target — e.g. cut usage by 25% each month. Identify your trigger moments (stress, boredom, after eating, social situations) — these are what you're really addressing, not just the nicotine
    - **Phase 2 — Quit Date (Month 4):** Set a firm quit date. Consider NRT (nicotine replacement therapy — patches, gum, or lozenges) to manage withdrawal while removing the smoking/vaping behaviour itself. Speak to a GP — prescription options like Champix/varenicline have strong success rates and are worth discussing
    - **Phase 3 — Protect the quit (Month 5–12):** Identify your highest-risk relapse moments and have a specific plan for each one. One slip is not a failure — it's data. Get back on track the same day
- **Visualise it:** Picture being in the gym 6 months from now with noticeably better lung capacity, recovering faster between sets, and knowing your body is actually working _with_ you instead of against you. Picture the money saved — calculate your monthly spend on cigarettes and vaping right now and imagine what that figure looks like across 12 months.
- **Potential problem 1 — Stress triggering relapse:** This is the most common one, especially combined with low mood and burnout. Pre-plan a replacement stress response — a walk outside, a breathing exercise, a 2 minute voice note rant to yourself. Something that uses the same "exit from overwhelm" function that smoking served.
- **Potential problem 2 — Social situations:** If smoking or vaping is tied to social settings, have a prop ready — a drink, gum, something in your hands. The ritual is often as addictive as the nicotine itself.
- **Reward:** Calculate exactly how much you spend on smoking and vaping per month. Every month you stay quit, move that exact amount into a separate account or pot labelled something meaningful — gear fund, holiday, studio equipment. Watching that number grow is one of the most motivating quit tools there is.
